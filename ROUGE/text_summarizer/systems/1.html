[1] The closest large neighbouring star to Sirius is Procyon, 1.61 parsecs (5.24ly) away. [2] Further north, the Alaskan Inuit of the Bering Strait called it "Moon Dog". [3] From there is it sent via the Sun to the god of Earth (Sanat Kumara), and finally through the seven Masters of the Seven Rays to the human race. [4] However, it is not as bright as the Moon, Venus, or Jupiter. [5] The star has the Bayer designation Alpha Canis Majoris ( CMa). [6] A similar association is depicted at the Temple of Hathor in Dendera, where the goddess Satet has drawn her arrow at Hathor (Sirius). [7] The most commonly used proper name of this star comes from the Latin Sirius, from the Ancient Greek (Seirios, "glowing" or "scorcher"), although the Greek word itself may have been imported from elsewhere before the Archaic period, one authority suggesting a link with the Egyptian god Osiris. [8] The bright stars Aldebaran, Arcturus and Sirius were noted to have moved significantly, the last of which having progressed 30 arc minutes (about the diameter of the moon) southwards in 1,800 years. [9] At a distance of 2.6 parsecs (8.6ly), the Sirius system contains two of the eight nearest stars to the Solar System and is the fifth closest stellar system to ours. [10] Due to precession (and slightly proper motion), Sirius will move further south.
